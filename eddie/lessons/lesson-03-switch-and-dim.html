Lesson 03
Switch It, Then Dim It

TASK — Turn the 12 V light bar on and off with the Pico. Then do it again with no moving parts. Then take a knob and make it fade.
CONDITIONS — Bench 12 V supply. Pico, relay module, MOSFET module, 10K pot, LED light bar. Thonny on the laptop, AI tutor on your phone.
STANDARD — Light blinks on command through the relay. Same blink through the MOSFET. Knob smoothly fades the bar 0–100% with no flicker. Explain the difference between a switch and a dimmer at debrief.

When I show a photo or an error, ask me diagnostic questions one at a time instead of answering. Keep replies short. If I try to get you to just solve it, remind me of this deal.
The big idea: the Pico is a tiny brain. It can push about 3.3 volts and a few thousandths of an amp — enough to light one little LED and nothing more. That light bar wants 12 volts and a lot more current. So the Pico never touches the light. It touches a switch, and the switch touches the light. Everything you build from here follows that rule: small signal commands, big thing obeys.

⚠ Safety Brief

  • Everything on this bench is 12 V or less. That is friendly. The worst thing that happens here is a part dies and we buy another one. That's tuition, not a hazard.
  • Wire it with the 12 V supply OFF. Build the circuit, look at it, then power it. Every time.
  • The light bar gets warm. That's normal. Hot enough you can't hold it means something's wrong — kill power and ask.
  • Watch polarity on the light bar. Red is +, black is −. Backwards usually just means "doesn't light," but don't push your luck.
  • If you smell anything or see smoke, pull the 12 V first. The Pico's USB is harmless. The 12 V rail is the muscle.

The four stages of this lesson

Same job, four times, each way better than the last. You are going to turn one light on and off with:

  1. A relay — a mechanical switch the Pico can click. You'll hear it.
  2. A MOSFET — the same job, silently, with no moving parts.
  3. A pot — the Pico learns to listen to a knob instead of just talk.
  4. All three together — the knob fades the light. That's PWM, and you already met it.

Do not skip ahead. Each stage is a different idea wearing the same job.

Stage 1 — The Relay

A relay is an electric switch with a little arm inside. Send it a signal and a coil pulls the arm over, click, and the big circuit closes. It's the same as you flipping a light switch — except the Pico's finger is a wire.

Wiring — Stage 1

WireFrom → ToWhy
Relay VCCPico VBUS (pin 40)The relay coil wants 5 V. VBUS is the 5 V coming in from USB.
Relay GNDPico GNDShared ground. The signal means nothing without it.

12 V +Relay COMPower comes into the switch here.
Relay NOLight bar red (+)NO = Normally Open. Off until the relay clicks.
12 V −Light bar black (−)The return path, straight back to the supply.

Heads up: the 12 V never touches the Pico. Look at the table again and prove that to yourself. Two separate worlds, joined only by a switch.

# Stage 1: click the relay on and off. The light follows.
#
# The Pico can't power a light bar - it can barely light one LED.
# So it doesn't try. It just tells a relay "close" and "open",
# and the relay does the heavy lifting with the 12 V.

from machine import Pin
import time

# GP16 is our command wire out to the relay's IN terminal.
relay = Pin(16, Pin.OUT)

print("Blinking the light bar. Listen for the click.")

while True:                  # loop forever until you hit Stop
    relay.value(1)           # signal HIGH  -> relay closes -> light ON
    print("ON  - click")
    time.sleep(1)            # leave it on for one second

    relay.value(0)           # signal LOW   -> relay opens  -> light OFF
    print("OFF - clack")
    time.sleep(1)            # leave it off for one second
CHECKPOINT — The light blinks once per second and you can hear the relay click each time. If the Shell prints but nothing happens, the problem is in the wiring, not the code. If it clicks but no light, the problem is on the 12 V side.

Now break it on purpose: change both time.sleep(1) to time.sleep(0.05) and run it. Listen. That poor relay is trying to physically slam a metal arm back and forth 20 times a second. It sounds awful, it will wear out, and it can't keep up.

That noise is the whole reason Stage 2 exists. A relay is a real switch with a real arm that has to physically move. It's great for on and off. It is terrible at doing that fast. Remember that sound — you're about to replace it with something that can switch thousands of times a second and never make a sound.
Stage 2 — The MOSFET (same job, no moving parts)

Pull the relay off the bench. Start over. Same light, same job, different switch.

A MOSFET is a switch with no arm, no coil, and nothing that moves. It's a chunk of silicon that either lets current through or doesn't, depending on the voltage you put on its control leg. No click. No wear. And it can switch fast.

Wiring — Stage 2

WireFrom → ToWhy
Board TRIG/PWMPico GP17The command wire. Same idea as the relay's IN.
Board GNDPico GNDShared ground — still required, always.
12 V +Blue terminal V+ (input side)Supply into the board.
12 V −Blue terminal V− (input side)Supply return.
Output +Light bar red (+)Switched power out to the load.
Output −Light bar black (−)The MOSFET actually switches this side.

Note the board has an LED on it that lights when it's triggered. Free diagnostic — if that LED is on and your light bar isn't, your problem is downstream.

# Stage 2: same blink, no relay. The MOSFET does it silently.
#
# Notice this code is almost identical to Stage 1. That's the point.
# From the Pico's side, nothing changed - it's still just setting
# a pin HIGH and LOW. Only the hardware listening to it changed.

from machine import Pin
import time

# GP17 goes to the MOSFET board's TRIG/PWM terminal.
gate = Pin(17, Pin.OUT)

print("Blinking through the MOSFET. Silence is the feature.")

while True:
    gate.value(1)            # HIGH -> MOSFET conducts -> light ON
    print("ON")
    time.sleep(1)

    gate.value(0)            # LOW  -> MOSFET blocks   -> light OFF
    print("OFF")
    time.sleep(1)
CHECKPOINT — Same blink, zero noise. Now run the 0.05 version again. The relay screamed. The MOSFET doesn't care. It'll do this all day, faster than you can see, and never wear out.

Notice what just happened: your code barely changed. Pin HIGH, pin LOW. The Pico doesn't know or care whether there's a relay or a MOSFET on the other end. That's a channel. You send a signal; something on the other end turns it into the real world. Swap the something, keep the code.

Stage 3 — The Pot (the Pico learns to listen)

Everything you've done so far, the Pico has been talking. Now it learns to listen.

A potentiometer — a pot — is a knob with a strip of resistance inside and a slider that rides along it. Put 3.3 V on one end and ground on the other, and the slider picks off a voltage somewhere between. Turn the knob, the voltage changes. That's it. That's the whole part.

Which wire is which? Your pot has three wires and no instruction sheet. Don't guess — measure. Meter on resistance:

  • The two wires whose resistance doesn't change when you turn the knob are the ends of the strip. (This pot is 10K, so expect about 10,000 ohms between them.)
  • The wire whose resistance changes as you turn is the wiper — the slider. That's the one that matters.

Do this before you wire anything. Finding a pinout with a meter is a real technician skill and you just used it.

WireToWhy

⚠ One hard rule on this stage

The pot goes on 3V3. Never on VBUS, never on 12 V. The Pico's reading pins max out at 3.3 volts. Feed them more and you kill the chip. This is the one mistake on this bench that costs you a Pico — and it's a wire in the wrong hole, nothing more.

# Stage 3: read the knob. No light yet - just listening.
#
# ADC means Analog-to-Digital Converter. It's the part of the Pico
# that measures a voltage and hands you back a number.
# 0 volts reads as 0.  3.3 volts reads as 65535.  Everything
# in between is proportional.

from machine import ADC, Pin
import time

# GP26 is ADC channel 0. Only GP26, GP27, GP28 can do this.
knob = ADC(Pin(26))

print("Turn the knob. Watch the number move.")

while True:
    raw = knob.read_u16()        # get the number: 0 to 65535

    # That number is big and ugly. Turn it into a percent
    # so it means something to a human.
    percent = raw * 100 // 65535

    print("raw:", raw, "  ->", percent, "%")
    time.sleep(0.2)              # 5 readings a second is plenty to watch
CHECKPOINT — Knob all the way down reads near 0. All the way up reads near 65535. Sweep it slowly and watch the number climb. You will probably not hit exactly 0 or exactly 65535, and the number will jitter a little even when you're not touching it. That's not broken. That's the real world — more on that at debrief.

Stage 4 — Put It Together (the knob dims the light)

Keep the MOSFET wired from Stage 2. Keep the pot wired from Stage 3. Now they meet.

Here's the trick, and you already know it from the motor: the MOSFET can't do half-on. It's a switch — on or off, nothing between. So how do you get a dim light? You blink it faster than the eye can see, and change how much of each blink is ON.

  • On 10% of the time, off 90% — looks dim.
  • On 90% of the time, off 10% — looks bright.
  • Do it a thousand times a second and your eye never sees the blinking. It just sees "dim" or "bright."

That's PWM. Pulse Width Modulation. It's the same thing you did to the motor in Lesson 01 — and the same discrete-time trick you watched in Lesson 02 when clicks turned into smooth motion. The light isn't dim. It's flickering, faster than you can catch it.

# Stage 4: the knob fades the light.
#
# Read the knob (a number 0-65535).
# Hand that same number straight to the PWM output.
# They happen to use the same range, which makes this almost too easy.

from machine import ADC, Pin, PWM
import time

knob = ADC(Pin(26))          # the pot's wiper - the input
light = PWM(Pin(17))         # the MOSFET's trigger - the output

# How many times per second we blink the light.
# 1000 is well past what your eye can see, so it looks smooth.
light.freq(1000)

print("Turn the knob. The light follows.")

try:
    while True:
        raw = knob.read_u16()     # 0 (knob down) to 65535 (knob up)

        light.duty_u16(raw)       # feed it straight to the light

        # Show a human-readable percent so you can watch it work.
        print(raw * 100 // 65535, "%")
        time.sleep(0.05)          # 20 updates a second - feels instant

finally:
    # Whatever happens - error, or you hitting Stop - turn it off.
    # Never leave a loop that drives something without this.
    light.duty_u16(0)
    print("Light off.")
CHECKPOINT — Knob down = dark. Knob up = full bright. Smooth all the way through, no flicker, no steps. Hit Stop — the light goes out, because finally: made sure of it.
Look at what you just built. A knob in your hand, a number in the Pico, and 12 volts doing what your fingers say. You didn't wire the knob to the light — you wired it to the Pico's ears, and the Pico told the MOSFET. That's the whole shape of every machine you'll ever build: read a thing, decide, act on a thing. Everything after this is just bigger nouns.

The pasted code can't pass this table. Author the changes yourself.
  1. Invert it. Make the knob work backwards — knob up = dark, knob down = bright. (One line. Think about what 65535 minus your reading gives you.)
  2. Floor and ceiling. Make the light never go fully dark and never go fully bright — hold it between about 10% and 90% no matter where the knob is.
